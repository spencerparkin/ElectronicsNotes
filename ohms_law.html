Ohm's Law

Here we reveal a fundamental relationship between voltage, current and resistance.

The Resistor

Associated with each resistor is a constant \(R\) denoting its resistance to current. (Variable resistors work by mechanically changing how far current has to travel through some sort of resistive channel.) Current through and voltage across a resistor may change over time, but their relationship is always governed by the equation... $$ V = IR. $$ This is Ohm's Law.

Note that all electronic components exhibit some resistance, so this is applicable to all components to some extent.

While on the subject, here we should discuss equivalents of Ohm's Law with respect to electronic components other than the resistor.

The Capacitor

Associated with each capacitor is a constant \(C\) denoting its capacitance. (Interestingly, this is purely a geometric property of the capacitor plates in terms of surface area, how far apart they are, how much they overlap, etc. Variable capacitors probably operate by mechanically changing the overlap.) The charge \(Q\) on a capacitor and the voltage \(V\) across the capacitor can change over time, but their relationship is always governed by the equation... $$ Q = CV. $$ But to get something analogous to Ohm's Law, we need to relate voltage and current with respect to the capacitor. Letting \(I\) and \(Q\) be functions of time \(t\), we need to add the equation... $$ \frac{d}{dt}Q(t) = \pm I(t), $$ ...but what is this saying? Well, capacitors can charge and discharge over time, and they do that when we pass a current through them using a battery, or let a current flow from them, respectively. What the equation says is that the rate of change of charge in the capacitor is directly proportional to the current flowing through/from it. This makes sense, because we can imaging electron build-up on the plates as current flows through the capacitor, or electron depletion as current flows from the capacitor. I'm not yet sure, however, how to describe the difference between charge and voltage in physical terms.
