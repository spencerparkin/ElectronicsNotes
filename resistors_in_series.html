Resistors in Series

Refering to the following circuit diagram...

...we let \(\{R_i\}_{i=1}^N\) be a set of \(N\) resistors in series, and let \(V_{bat}\) denote the total voltage of the battery. These are our known values. Unknown will be the values in \(\{V_i\}_{i=1}^N\), the voltage drops across each resistor, \(\{I_i\}_{i=1}^N\), the currents running through each resistor, \(R_{eqv}\), the equivalent resistance of all the resistors, and \(I\), the net current flowing through the simple circuit. Note that the battery has been split apart to make the diagram simpler.

Our first observation here is an application of Kirchhoff's second law. Being made, we realize that for all \(i\in[1,N]\), we have \(I=I_i\). Why? Because there is just one path through the entire circuit, and currents change only when paths split or converge. That's the nature of current.

Applying Ohm's law, we can now find that \(V_i=IR_i=V_{bat}R_i/R_{eqv}\), leaving \(R_{eqv}\) as all we yet need to deduce to determine all the voltage drops.

Using the equation \(V_{bat}=IR_{eqv}\) and the set of equations \(\{V_i=I_iR_i\}_{i=1}^N\), we get... $$ IR_{eqv} = V_{bat}=\sum_{i=1}^N V_i = \sum_{i=1}^N IR_i = I\sum_{i=1}^N R_i. $$ Now canceling \(I\) on both sides gives us our main result; namely, $$ R_{eqv} = \sum_{i=1}^N R_i. $$

The major take-home point here is that the equivilant resistence of resistors in series is simply the sum of all the resistances of the resistors.

Note that if I were using variables representing voltages at points instead of voltages across resistors, then we would have witnessed a telescoping sum in the math.
