The Physics & Analogous Models

Circuits involving nothing more than batteries and resistors are fundamental to the study of electronics, because we need a power source, all electronic components have some internal resistance (or the opposite), and we must study steady-state (or static) electrical systems before we get into dynamical systems.

Reality

My understanding is that atoms are made of electrons and protons, and they like to have an equal number of each. Atoms with an excess of electrons (i.e., more electrons than protons) want to get rid of some while those with a deficit (i.e., less electrons than protons) want to gain some. The exchange of electrons from one atom to another to appease these demands constitutes what we call electric current.

Atoms of various kinds are more or less willing to give up or gain electrons. Those more willing are conductors of electric current, and those less are insulators.

An atom with more electrons than protons is said to be negatively charged, while an atom with less electrons than protons is said to be negatively charged. A source of electrical energy (e.g., a battery) provides positive and negative terminals at which can be found atoms of the same charge. When a conductive path is completed from one terminal to another, current can and does want to flow.

One of the laws of physics is that energy is neither created nor destroyed. The electrical energy from the battery is converted to other forms of energy by the components in an electrical circuit, such as light, heat and sound.

Intuition

A useful model/analogy1 of electron flow in a circuit is to think of marbles and tracks. A battery is like an elevator that continuously takes marbles up to the top and dumps them down a track. The pathways of the circuit are tracks the marbles can roll down. These tracks split and converge as the circuit diagram dictates until they eventually arrive at the bottom of the elevator.

Voltage at a point on a track in the circuit may be thought of as the height of that point above ground level. Current at a point may be thought of as the number of marbles (electrons) per second passing that point. Resistance may be thought of as the frictional properties of the track.

Note that voltage across two points is the difference in height between those two points.

Direction of Current Flow

A fundamental question of any circuit is that of which way current is flowing at any particular point. (Never-mind how much current is flowing for now.) The answer comes from our model of electronic circuits, and it is as simple as that current flows from places of high voltage to places of lower voltage. As far as how much, it suffices to say at this point that electrons are lazy. Most prefer the path of least resistance. Few are willing to take harder paths.

It is a bit distressing (or was for me) to learn that circuit diagrams are drawn and circuit analyses are performed using a convention of current flowing in the opposite direction from that of reality. In reality, current flows from the negative terminal of the battery to the positive. Conventionally, current flows from the positive terminal to the negative. What you must quickly learn is that in our analysis of some circuits, it almost doesn't matter. The symmetry of many circuit problems and components may be an explanation. Anyhow, I'm still weary of conventional current, and cannot yet see at this point how it won't matter for more complicated circuits containing more complicated types of components.
